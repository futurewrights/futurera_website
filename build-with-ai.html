About this course

Build with AI, not just use it.

AI is changing every field — and the students who can build with it, not just use it, will have an enormous advantage going forward. This course gives high schoolers exactly that skill set, with no prior coding experience required.

Students learn how AI actually works in plain English: what large language models really are, what they can do, and where they fall short. They learn to write prompts that make AI reliably useful — for school, creative projects, and everyday problems. They learn to generate real content with AI — writing, images, ideas, summaries, and simple code — and to choose the right tool for each task. They also learn to recognize the risks: hallucinations, bias, over-reliance, and the moments when human judgment still matters most. And they learn the ethics of using AI responsibly — privacy, intellectual property, academic honesty, and the line between helpful and dishonest use.

By the end of the course, every student has a portfolio of working AI products they built themselves — and the confidence to keep building on their own.

What students can build

Real apps. Real URLs. Real users.

Students put it all together by designing and shipping their own AI applications. Those who arrive with a clear idea build it; those who don't pick from a curated starter list. Nobody leaves without a finished, deployed product.

Course outcomes

By the end, students will be able to —

1

Build real AI products

Design and deploy working AI applications hosted at real public URLs. Not toy demos — real products their friends and family can use.

2

Write prompts & generate content

Write prompts that get AI to do what they want, and use AI fluently for writing, images, ideas, summaries, and code. The most useful AI skill, and the one most adults still don't have.

3

Understand how AI really works

Not the math, the plain-English version. What language models actually do, what they can't do, and where the line is.

4

Spot when AI is wrong

Recognize hallucinations — the confident, made-up answers AI sometimes gives — and know how to catch, verify, and correct them.

5

Use AI responsibly

Understand the real ethics: privacy, bias, intellectual property, and academic honesty. Know the line between helpful and dishonest use.

6

Pitch their work

Stand in front of a room (or a college admissions reader) and explain in 60 seconds what they built, how it works, and why it matters.

Tools used

All free. All transferable.

We use only free, publicly available tools so the entire course can be replicated by students at home, on their own laptop, after the course ends. The skills travel.
