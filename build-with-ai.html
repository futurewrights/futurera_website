Build with AI — From Prompt to Product.

A course where students aged 14–18 build and ship two real AI products with little to no code. Students leave with two live URLs, a 60-second pitch, and the ability to explain how AI actually works.

What students build

Two real AI products. Two live URLs.

Most school projects end up in the trash on the last day of class. These end up on the public internet. Students' friends can open them on their phones the moment they ship.

First product

A custom AI character

Students design the personality of their own chatbot — a Bestie, a Sensei, a coach in their favourite athlete's voice, an AP Chem tutor — and ship it as a real website with a shareable link. Then classmates try to break it, and the student patches it to hold up under pressure.

Second product

A personalized Study Buddy

Students upload their own school notes (PDFs, slides, Google Docs) and build a personalized AI tutor that answers questions and quizzes them on their own material. Most students pick a subject they're actually struggling with, so the tool ends up being something they keep using after the course ends.

The four concepts

The same four ideas every working AI engineer uses.

System prompts. Retrieval. Hallucinations. Deployment. The main difference between students at the end of this course and engineers at OpenAI or Anthropic is that the engineers write production code and the students work mostly with no-code and low-code tools. The concepts are identical.

01

System prompts

The hidden layer of instructions that turns a generic chatbot into a tutor, a coach, anything you design.

02

Retrieval-augmented generation

How to build an AI that only answers from your own notes, instead of making things up. How every serious AI product is built.

03

Hallucinations

When AI confidently says things that aren't true. The same techniques real engineers use to catch it before it ships.

04

Deployment

How to put a working AI tool at a public URL anyone can open. Sounds intimidating; takes about 20 minutes once it's designed well.

Course flow

Five modules. Same arc, every format.

The course follows the same five-module arc whether it's delivered as a 2-week bootcamp or a longer evening cohort. Content and outcomes don't change — only pacing.

01

Foundations: how AI prompts actually work

The difference between a system prompt and a user prompt. See firsthand how the same task phrased five ways produces five different outputs. The most useful skill in the course.

02

Build & ship: design and deploy your AI character

Pick a personality, write the system prompt, and ship the chatbot to a public URL anyone can open. The first product.

03

Stress-test: peer review, find the cracks, patch them

Classmates try to break each other's chatbots. Document the failures. Patch the prompts. The iteration loop that all real software goes through.

04

Retrieval: build a Study Buddy that knows your own notes

What an embedding is, in plain English. Retrieval-augmented generation explained. Build an AI tutor from your own school material, and stress-test it for hallucinations.

05

Show your work: write the explainer, deliver the pitch

One-page document in your own words. 60-second live pitch. A final showcase where a real audience tests the Study Buddy on real material.

Course outcomes

By the end, students will be able to —

1

Build, not just use

Build and deploy a working AI chatbot with their own custom personality, hosted on a real public URL anyone can open.

2

Trust AI when it earns it

Build a personalized AI study tool that answers only from their own notes, and explain why that's safer than asking ChatGPT.

3

Write prompts that actually work

Get an AI to do what they want on the first or second try, not the tenth. The single most useful skill we teach.

4

Spot when AI is wrong

Recognize hallucinations — confident, made-up answers — and know how to catch and correct them.

5

Pitch their work

Stand in front of a room (or a college admissions reader) and explain in 60 seconds what they built and why it matters.

6

Explain how AI works

Not the math — the plain-English version. What "training" means. What an embedding is, in a way that makes sense.

Tools used
