For parents

The questions you're already asking, answered honestly.

You are right to be careful. The internet is loud, the technology is moving quickly, and most "AI for kids" courses you'll find online are thin. Here's what we'll commit to — in plain language, without the marketing.

Many AI programs promise transformation; few can show what a student actually leaves with. Below is the honest version: what your child will do, what we won't claim, and the case for the Build with AI course specifically.

What we'll commit to

Your child will leave Futurera with a real portfolio: projects you can hand to a university admissions officer or a co-op interviewer and have a substantive conversation about. Their instructor will be a qualified educator paired with a working practitioner, not a generic tutor following a script. The curriculum is aligned with Ontario Ministry expectations, so it complements school rather than competing with it.

Cohort sizes are set per program, small enough that students get weekly 1:1 feedback from their mentor. And if your child isn't a "tech kid," that's fine. AI Lab is built around using AI well in any subject.

If after reading this you're still unsure, that is a good reason to book a 20-minute conversation. We will tell you honestly whether your child is ready for our program now, in six months, or whether something else is the right fit.

If you're considering Build with AI — From Prompt to Product

Build with AI — From Prompt to Product is the first AI Lab course. The Summer 2026 cohort is a 2-week intensive in Toronto. Here's the case for it.

Why your child needs to take this course

Your child already uses ChatGPT. So does every other student in their grade. The fact that a teenager can ask a chatbot a question and copy the answer is not a skill anymore. It's a baseline, like knowing how to Google.

What is becoming an actual skill is the ability to build with AI: knowing what's happening underneath ChatGPT, writing instructions that get the right answer on the first try, knowing when an AI is making something up and how to stop it, and putting a working AI tool on the public internet that another person can use.

Almost no high school teaches any of this. Most schools have either banned AI outright or reduced it to "do not use it on essays." Two weeks in this course closes a gap the school system isn't closing. By the end, your child will have built two working AI products and will be able to explain how they work in plain language. That's a credential almost no other applicant in their college pool will have.

Why these skills matter right now

The four concepts your child learns are not tied to any one company or any one tool. System prompts, retrieval-augmented generation, hallucination detection, and deployment are the architecture of every modern AI product on the market. They're how Microsoft Copilot is built. They're how every legal research AI, medical summarization tool, and customer support copilot is built.

A teenager who can explain how a system prompt shapes AI behaviour is in a tiny minority of AI-literate teenagers in the country. A teenager who can explain what an embedding is, in plain English, has cleared a conceptual bar that most working adults haven't.

When your child applies to college in two or three years, almost every applicant will claim to use AI in some capacity. Almost none will be able to attach two working URLs to their application and explain how they built them. That's the difference between participating in the AI conversation and contributing to it.

Why this is both educational and entertaining

Students aren't assigned a project. They invent the character their chatbot becomes. They pick the school subject their Study Buddy is built around. They decide the tone, the name, and the first message. The student is the one making the creative choices, which is the difference between work and play for almost every teenager.

The stress-test day is the social day. Classmates try to break each other's chatbots on purpose and document the failures. Competitive, funny, educational at the same time. The stress-test results are the lesson, but they come wrapped in a real game played among real friends.

Showcase day has stakes. Parents arrive, students give a 60-second pitch, and parents try the Study Buddy on their child's actual homework in real time. The day is not quiet.

Why students will actually enjoy it

Almost every "educational" summer activity for teenagers is built around a project the student secretly knows is a toy. This program is built around two products with public URLs that the student's friends can open on their phones the moment they ship. That difference changes the entire motivation curve.

The mixed-skill room is part of the design. Beginners and code-curious students work side by side and learn from each other. No student is bored, and no student is lost.

The output is real, the deadlines are real, and the audience on the last day is real. For most teenagers, this is the first time school feels like building something rather than completing something.
