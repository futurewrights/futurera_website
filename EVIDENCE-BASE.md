# The AI Transformation in Education and Work — Evidence Base for the Program Pitch

**Prepared:** May 2026 · Sources current as of search date

This brief gathers the public research and reporting that supports the three core
arguments of our AI Education for High Schoolers program:

1. We are moving from a digital **consumer** era to an AI **builder** era.
2. The job market and university gate have already shifted — entry-level work is
   contracting, and AI fluency is now an entry expectation.
3. K–12 education is not yet structured to prepare students for either reality.

---

## 1. From the app era to the AI era: consumer → builder

The most well-documented behavioral shift in this cohort is how Gen Z is using AI.
Roughly **80% of Gen Z** (born 1997–2012) use AI tools daily, and **61%** specifically
use them to support learning [1]. They are not waiting for school systems to introduce
AI; they are already using ChatGPT, Midjourney, Copilot, and similar tools to write,
design, code, and ship — including, in a growing number of cases, building real products
and companies before they finish high school [2][3].

The framing question for educators is no longer "should students use AI?" but "are they
using it to **consume**, or to **create**?" Industry commentary and curriculum-design
research both argue that the educational task is to push students toward the second
posture — building with AI as a co-pilot, not just chatting with it [2][4]. Stanford
Digital Education has launched a high-school AI curriculum on exactly this premise:
students should understand how the models work and use them to build [5][6].

## 2. The job market has already moved — and it expects AI fluency

**Entry-level work is contracting**

- Workers aged 22–25 in AI-exposed roles (software, customer service, accounting)
  experienced a **16% relative drop** in employment in under three years [7].
- Job postings targeting students and recent graduates fell **16%** while applications
  per opening rose **26%** — the most pessimistic employer outlook for new graduates
  since 2020 (NACE Job Outlook 2026) [7][8].
- **80% of college seniors** in the class of 2026 say AI is reducing entry-level
  opportunities, even as **67%** expect AI skills to raise their own pay [9].

**Employer expectations have stepped up**

- By automating the easy on-the-job learning work, AI has pushed employers to expect
  graduates to be "job-ready" at a mid-level from day one [7][8].
- Familiarity with AI tools is now treated as a **baseline expectation** in most
  knowledge-work fields; the durable differentiators are judgment, communication,
  ethical reasoning, and domain depth [7].

**The macro picture is disruption, not extinction**

- The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025 projects **92 million jobs
  displaced and 170 million created by 2030** — a net gain, but a sharp reshuffle [10].
- AI and big data sit at the top of the WEF's most-in-demand tech skills list, alongside
  creative and critical thinking and lifelong learning as the most-in-demand human
  skills [10].

## 3. K–12 education has not caught up

- **96%** of U.S. K–12 teachers report receiving no professional development on AI from
  their school or district [11].
- **85%** of educators feel unprepared to manage generative AI in their classrooms;
  **32%** say they are completely unprepared [11].
- As of late 2024, only **14%** of U.S. public K–12 schools reported teaching students
  about ethical and appropriate AI use [11].
- Only **51%** of recent graduates believed they had sufficient AI skills for the jobs
  they applied to (Cengage 2025 Graduate Employability Report) [11].
- Most institutions still lack formal AI policies even as student use of AI is
  near-universal [12].

The combined picture is a **structural mismatch**. Students are already builders with AI
in their personal lives, employers already expect them to arrive with mid-level AI
fluency, and universities are starting to read for AI-built portfolios — but the
classroom infrastructure to develop those skills systematically does not yet exist at
scale.

## 4. University admissions are quietly shifting

- Portfolios — once reserved for art and CS applicants — are increasingly used across
  majors as digital evidence of curiosity and initiative; ML models, automation
  projects, and AI-built tools are explicitly cited as strong submissions [13].
- Caltech is piloting an AI tool that interviews students on video about projects they
  submit, to assess the authenticity of the work [13].
- Roughly **1 in 3** college applicants reported using AI to help with essays in the 2025
  cycle, and admissions offices are tightening rules about disclosure and AI
  detection [13].
- For families targeting selective programs in CS, AI, or STEM, a portfolio of original
  AI-built work is moving from nice-to-have to **expected**.

## 5. Implications for our program design

The research supports each of the three program levels:

- **Foundations** exists because Gen Z is using AI daily without being taught how the
  models actually work — closing that gap is the first lever for shifting students from
  consumers to builders.
- **Specialization** exists because both gates that follow high school — universities and
  employers — have moved their expectations forward, and require demonstrable
  AI-fluency evidence.
- **AI Studio** exists because the research consistently points to project-based
  learning, real industry problems, and an entrepreneurial posture as the most effective
  bridge between AI literacy and the kind of track record students now need.
