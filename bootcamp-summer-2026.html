What makes this cohort different

A small, in-person cohort — not a self-paced video.

Different from the YouTube AI courses parents have seen advertised. Taught in real time by senior faculty on our core team, with a stress-test day where students break each other's work and a Showcase Day where parents test the result on their own child's homework.

For students

If you're a high schooler reading this — read this.

A note for you, in your own voice —

Why you should take this course

You already know how to ask ChatGPT a question. Everyone in your school does. That's not a skill anymore — it's a baseline, like knowing how to Google.

What is a skill is being able to build with AI. That means writing the kind of instructions that get AI to do what you want on the first try, knowing what's happening under the chat window, knowing when an AI is lying to you, and putting a working AI tool at a public URL your friends can open on their phones.

Almost nobody in your grade can do any of this yet. Most teachers can't either. Two weeks in this course puts you in a category of person that companies, colleges, and the rest of the internet are going to keep paying attention to for the rest of your life.

Why this matters right now

The four ideas you learn here are the same four ideas the engineers at OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google work with every single day. The only difference between you and them at the end of two weeks is that they wrote code and you used no-code tools. The concepts are identical.

In five years, "I use AI" will mean nothing. Everyone will use it. The people who actually go somewhere will be the people who can explain how it works and build their own version of it. That second category starts here, at age 14 to 18.

When you apply to college in two or three years, you'll be competing against people who claim they "used AI for an essay." You'll be the one with two live URLs to attach to your application.

What you'll actually do

You design the personality of your chatbot. You pick the subject your Study Buddy is built around. You decide what it sounds like, what it refuses to answer, what its name is, what its first message says. The work is yours.

There's a stress-test day where you spend the whole session trying to break your friends' chatbots on purpose, and they spend it trying to break yours. You document where the cracks are. This is competitive, funny, and the actual lesson at the same time.

On the final day, your parents come in and test your Study Buddy on your real homework in front of you. The room is loud. There are stakes.

Why you'll actually enjoy this

Most school projects end up in the trash on the last day of class. This one ends up at a URL that lives on the public internet for as long as you want it to. Your friends can open it on their phones. Your siblings can use it. You can keep using it after camp ends.

The two weeks are spent playing with the best AI tools that exist right now, in a structured way, with someone in the room who knows how to use them. You're not figuring this out alone in your bedroom at midnight.

You design what you build. You break your friends' work on a Friday. You walk out with two live URLs that work. That's a different kind of summer.

For parents

The case for parents — five concrete questions, answered.

If you're a parent considering this course —

Why your child needs to take this course

Your child already uses ChatGPT. So does every other student in their grade. The fact that a teenager can ask a chatbot a question and copy the answer is not a skill anymore — it's a baseline, like knowing how to Google.

What is becoming an actual skill is the ability to build with AI: knowing what's happening underneath ChatGPT, writing instructions that get the right answer on the first try, knowing when an AI is making something up and how to stop it, and putting a working AI tool on the public internet that another person can use.

Almost no high school teaches any of this. Most schools have either banned AI outright or reduced it to "do not use it on essays." Two weeks in this course closes a gap the school system isn't closing. By the end, your child will have built two working AI products and will be able to explain how they work in plain language. That's a credential almost no other applicant in their college pool will have.

Why these skills matter right now

The four concepts your child learns are not tied to any one company or any one tool. System prompts, retrieval-augmented generation, hallucination detection, and deployment are the architecture of every modern AI product on the market. They're how Microsoft Copilot is built. They're how every legal research AI, medical summarization tool, and customer support copilot is built.

A teenager who can explain how a system prompt shapes AI behaviour is in a tiny minority of AI-literate teenagers in the country. A teenager who can explain what an embedding is, in plain English, has cleared a conceptual bar that most working adults haven't.

When your child applies to college in two or three years, almost every applicant will claim to use AI in some capacity. Almost none will be able to attach two working URLs to their application and explain how they built them. That's the difference between participating in the AI conversation and contributing to it.

Why this is both educational and entertaining

Students aren't assigned a project. They invent the character their chatbot becomes. They pick the school subject their Study Buddy is built around. They decide the tone, the name, and the first message. The student is the one making the creative choices — which is the difference between work and play for almost every teenager.

The stress-test day is the social day. Classmates try to break each other's chatbots on purpose and document the failures. Competitive, funny, educational at the same time. The stress-test results are the lesson, but they come wrapped in a real game played among real friends.

Showcase day has stakes. Parents arrive, students give a 60-second pitch, and parents try the Study Buddy on their child's actual homework in real time. The day is not quiet.

Why students will actually enjoy it

Almost every "educational" summer activity for teenagers is built around a project the student secretly knows is a toy. This program is built around two products with public URLs that the student's friends can open on their phones the moment they ship. That difference changes the entire motivation curve.

The mixed-skill room is part of the design. Beginners and code-curious students work side by side and learn from each other. No student is bored, and no student is lost.

The output is real, the deadlines are real, and the audience on the last day is real. For most teenagers, this is the first time school feels like building something rather than completing something.

Day-by-day

Week 1: Build the chatbot · Week 2: Build the Study Buddy.

Two real products, ten days, three hours each. Every student leaves with two live URLs, a 60-second pitch, and a one-page explainer they wrote themselves.

1

Week 1 — Custom AI character

Design and ship a chatbot with its own personality. Then break each other's bots on Friday.
Day 1 · Mon

System prompts vs. user prompts

The hidden layer that turns a generic chatbot into anything. Hands-on: the same task, phrased five ways, gets five answers.

Day 2 · Tue

Design your AI character

Pick a personality — a Bestie, a Sensei, a coach, an AP Chem tutor. Write the system prompt. Decide tone, name, first message.

Day 3 · Wed

Ship to a public URL

Deploy your chatbot to a real, shareable URL. Friends can open it on their phones the moment it's live.

Day 4 · Thu

Break each other's bots

The social day. Try to break your classmates' chatbots on purpose. Document the cracks. Patch your own.

Day 5 · Fri

Polish + reflect

Lock in v1 of the chatbot. Reflect on what worked. Set up Week 2 — the personalized Study Buddy.

2

Week 2 — Personalized Study Buddy

Build an AI tutor that knows your own school notes. Showcase it on Day 10.
Day 6 · Mon

Retrieval-augmented generation

What an embedding is, in plain English. Why ChatGPT can't answer questions about your homework — and how RAG fixes that.

Day 7 · Tue

Build the Study Buddy

Upload your own notes (PDFs, slides, docs). Build an AI tutor that answers from your material and quizzes you on it.

Day 8 · Wed

Hunt hallucinations

The same techniques production engineers use to catch AI lies. Stress-test your Study Buddy. Patch the gaps.

Day 9 · Thu

Write the explainer

One page, in your own words. Name the system prompt, the retrieval step, the model. This is what goes on a college application.

Day 10 · Fri

Showcase Day

60-second pitch + live demo. Parents arrive and test the Study Buddy on your real homework in front of the room.

Honest framing

What we don't promise.

Every course on the internet over-promises. Here's what we won't claim.

What we won't claim

  • That your child will become an AI engineer in two weeks
  • That they'll launch a startup
  • That the specific tools they use today will be the same in a year — AI is moving fast and the software will keep changing

What we do promise

  • Your child will learn the underlying skill — how to think about AI, design with it, test it, ship something real
  • That skill outlives any specific tool
  • That skill is what employers, colleges, and the world will keep paying for
Bootcamp FAQ

Specific questions for this cohort.

For the full course philosophy, see the Build with AI course page. For broader questions about AI Lab, see the AI Lab page or the general FAQ.

Does my child need a coding background?

No. The bootcamp uses no-code tools throughout — students don't write a line of code. The skills they learn (system prompts, retrieval-augmented generation, deployment) are conceptually identical to what working AI engineers do, just with a different interface. There's also an optional code track for code-curious students; both run side-by-side in the same room.

What does my child need to bring?

A laptop. Any modern model works — Chromebook, Mac, Windows. That's it. All software is free and runs in the browser. If a laptop is a barrier for your family, mention it on the info call; we can usually arrange a loaner for the bootcamp duration.

What tools will my child use?

All free, all publicly available: Lovable, Google NotebookLM, Claude, Bolt.new, Vercel. The deliberate choice is that your child can keep using these tools at home after camp ends — the skills travel.

If your child opts into the optional code track, there's a small $10–15 tools fee that covers API credits.

Where in Toronto does it run?

The exact venue will be confirmed with applicants in May 2026 — typically central Toronto, transit-accessible, with a real classroom space (not a hotel ballroom). tbd

What if my child misses a day?

We provide a recap and a 1:1 catch-up session with a mentor. The bootcamp is intensive — we strongly encourage full attendance, but life happens, and we don't penalize for one missed day. Missing more than two days is usually a sign the timing isn't right; we'd rather defer enrollment than rush.

Is parent attendance required or invited?

Not required. Parents are warmly invited to Showcase Day on Day 10 — your child gives a 60-second pitch and then you test their Study Buddy on their actual chemistry or history homework, in front of the room. The day is loud. There are stakes. It's the moment that makes the whole two weeks make sense.

What's the application process?

A short written application (a few sentences about why your child wants to take it and what they'd hope to build), then a 15-minute conversation with our program lead. Decisions within five business days. Rolling intake until the cohort fills.

What does it cost?

Tuition is discussed on the info call. Need-based scholarships are available; mention this when you reach out — we mean it, it's not a token program. price tbd

Refund policy?

Full refund up to two weeks before the bootcamp starts. 50% refund up to one week before. After that, no refund except for documented medical reasons. Details on the application form.

Do you issue a certificate of completion?

We don't. Most short-form AI courses do, and most of those certificates aren't worth much. What your child leaves with — two live URLs, a 60-second pitch they've delivered, and a one-page explainer they wrote — is harder to fake and more useful in any college application, interview, or real-world setting.

If a verification record is needed for a school program or scholarship, we'll provide one on request, signed by the lead instructor.

What happens after the bootcamp?

The chatbot and Study Buddy your child builds keep running on their public URLs after the bootcamp ends — friends can use them, siblings can use them, your child can keep iterating. Many students continue using their Study Buddy through the school year for the subject they built it around.
